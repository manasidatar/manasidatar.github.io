Shapes with Nonregular Surfaces

methods for robust correspondences

Isotropy invariant correspondences via relative distances

To address the challenges posed by the inherent complexity (e.g. cortical surface shapes) and variability (e.g. cardiac chambers) evident in many biomedical shapes, we propose the use of geodesic distances to a-priori landmarks as features in the correspondence optimization process. The proposed method minimizes the ensemble entropy based on these features, resulting in isometry invariant correspondences in a very general, flexible framework.

Improved correspondences via feature entropy

To resolve the challenges posed by highly nonregular surfaces, we have proposed an efficient method which incorporates Geodesic distances and an intershape penalty based on surface normals to improve correspondences.

Correspondences for open surfaces

To compute correspondence positions on a set of open surfaces, we define the boundary as the intersection of the surface with a set of geometric primitives. Our goal is to formulate the interactions with these boundaries so that the positions of these constraints has as little influence as possible on the statistical shape model. [w/ J Cates]

FAI analysis for femur (application)

Cam femoroacetabular impingement (FAI) is characterized by a malformed femoral head that may lead to early hip osteoarthritis. Statistical shape modeling (SSM) is be used to compare and quantify morphologic differences between control and FAI femurs. [w/ MD Harris]

FAI analysis for cartilage (application)

The aim of this study was to display shape differences in acetabular cartilage between controls and dysplasia patients to give a preview of how form (i.e., shallow acetabulum) relates to function (e.g., altered cartilage contact pressures).

Studying the short bone phenotype in osteochondroma (application)

A study of the 'steal phenomenon' caused by multiple osteochondromas in mouse models was performed by comparing bone lengths and volumes and correlating bone length deviations with metaphyseal volume deviations from normal (as a measure of osteochondroma volumetric growth). [w/ KB Jones]

Study of shape changes related to ischemia (application)

A framework to study anatomic variability and perform hypothesis testing to quantify shape changes related to heart failure remodeling. [w/ Dr. R Winslow]

Shape change over time

methods for regression of correspondences

Linear shape regression

Correspondence based on linear regression was incorporated to arrive at a normative model of early head shape development. [w/ J Cates]

Linear mixed-effects model for shapes

Longitudinal shape changes in anatomy are characterized using a new method that combines point correspondences across shapes with the statistical modeling of individual and population trends via the linear mixed-effects (LME) model. [w/ P Murlidharan, A Kumar]

Atrial fibrillation: human study (application)

A proof-of-concept study of left atrium (LA) shape change after successful radiofrequency (RF) ablation. The results indicate that mixed-effects models could be used to uncover subtle shape changes beyond simple volume differences and may be used to investigate clinical hypotheses regarding LA shape and its relationship to AF. [w/ J Cates]

Atrial fibrillation: canine study (application)

An investigation of population trends in atrial shape change during AF in a canine model where the LME model consistently detects subtle atrial shape changes across groups in spite of variation in anatomy across individuals and can be leveraged for clinical assessment of AF progression. [w/ J Cates, J Jenson]

Huntington's Disease study (application)

The objective of this study was to make the LME shape model accessible for further exploration of Huntington’s Disease (HD) based on the longitudinal data made available by the PREDICT-HD study. [w/ P Muralidharan]

Machine learning methods

sample selection, image segmentation and then some...

Natural scene segmentation

Natural scene segmentation is carried out using a two-stage hierarchical self-organizing map (HSOM). The proposed method confirms that the sample selection based on homogeneity and the selflearning ability and adaptability of the HSOM, coupled with the information fusion mechanism (color and texture information), can lead to good segmentation results, which is validated by experiments on a variety of natural scene images.

Molecular pathology image segmentation

A tissue microarray with known disease stages can be used to enable efficient pathology slide image analysis. We focus on an intuitive approach for segmenting such images, using the Hierarchical Self-Organizing Map (HSOM). Our approach introduces the use of unsupervised clustering using both color and texture features, and the use of unsupervised color merging outside of the HSOM framework. An application to segmentation for prostrate cancer staging is presented.

Image orientation detection

We present an Automated Image Orientation Detection (AIOD) algorithm based on the supervised self-organizing map (SOM). The proposed system achieves decent accuracy with a compact feature vector, minimal training time, and also a small training set.

Multimodality image registration

methods to incorporate material properties, validation

Spatially variying degrees of freedom (DOF)

Intrasubject deformable registration applications use a high DOF deformation to accurately align soft tissue. However, smoothness constraints applied to the deformation may distort the more rigid tissue types. We present a technique that aligns tissue types based on spatially varying DOF.

Anatomically guided registration

Simple anatomically guided approach for whole body image registration offers enhanced alignment of large coverage inter-scan studies. In this experiment, we provide anatomy specific transformations to capture their independent motions.

Correlating perfusion/diffusion with anatomy

This was a clinical study designed to assess the performance of our multimodality registration algorithms in registering functional (PET/DWI) images with corresponding anatomical images (MRI). Quantitative assessment was done using point correspondences.
